While the city's source water is indeed naturally soft and high quality, the journey it takes through an aging distribution system, combined with seasonal runoff and localized plumbing issues, can introduce contaminants and affect taste and quality at your tap. This is why many Denver homeowners and businesses explore local water treatment solutions.

### Denver's Water Unfiltered: What's at Your Tap?

As communitywatertest.org often highlights, understanding your local water context is the first step toward better water. Denver Water, the city's primary provider, draws its supply from mountain snowmelt in the South Platte River Basin and the Colorado River Basin. This source water is inherently soft, meaning it has low concentrations of minerals like calcium and magnesium.

However, the "last mile" of water delivery can introduce variables. Older pipes in the city's infrastructure can leach sediment or trace minerals. Seasonal agricultural or urban runoff can introduce dissolved solids or chemicals that might not be fully removed by municipal treatment, or might bypass it entirely in private well situations. Even if your water is technically safe to drink, you might notice issues like an undesirable taste, odor, or cloudy appearance. For these reasons, many Denver residents consider in-home filtration.

*   **Whole House Filtration:** This is often the first line of defense against a broad spectrum of contaminants that can enter your home's water supply. *   **Reverse Osmosis (RO) Systems:** While whole-house filters are excellent for general improvement, RO systems are designed for superior drinking water quality. These point-of-use systems, typically installed under the kitchen sink, use a semi-permeable membrane to remove up to 99% of dissolved solids, including nitrates, lead, fluoride, and other microscopic impurities. For those in Denver concerned about specific contaminants or simply desiring the purest possible drinking and cooking water, an RO system is a popular choice.
